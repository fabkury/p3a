## Notes on the choices

- **Subject leads with "open-source $48 device" and the word "free"**
  — Open Culture's headline taxonomy uses these words constantly
  ("Free Online Course," "Free Download," "Open Access"). Matching
  their vocabulary increases the odds of sticking in editorial intake.
- **The "trifecta" framing** — "open hardware running open-source
  firmware against open cultural APIs" — is the load-bearing line.
  Open Culture's editors specifically respond to layered openness;
  any one of the three alone is just another tech tip.
- **"A reader could buy and have running by the weekend"** matters
  for OC because their audience is *doers* — they bookmark and try
  free/open things, not just read about them. Lowering the perceived
  effort barrier is part of the pitch.
- **"No cloud account in the loop"** is the anti-extraction signal,
  important for the open-content audience that's allergic to walled
  gardens.
- **No mention of IIIF, Linked Art, ESP32-P4 internals, or pixel art**
  — same logic as the Hyperallergic tip; the GitHub README carries
  the full identity. Pixel art in particular doesn't fit OC's beat.
- **No "radio for art" framing** even though it worked well for
  Hyperallergic — the framing here is open-stack, not democratization.
  Different angle for a different audience.
- **No "no ask" line** — same logic as Hyperallergic; the implicit ask
  is "run a tip," and pretending otherwise reads as dishonest.
- **The r/MuseumPros line (added 2026-06-11)** — same logic as the
  Hyperallergic tip: traction is evidence of audience appetite. One
  sentence, link included, numbers refreshed on send day.
